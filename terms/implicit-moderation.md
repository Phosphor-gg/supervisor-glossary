# Implicit Moderation

Implicit moderation is the practice of catching harmful content that is implied rather than stated outright. Instead of looking for a banned word or phrase, it evaluates what a message actually means in context, including insinuation, coded language, sarcasm, and indirect threats.

## What it means

Most harmful messages do not contain an obvious slur or keyword. People who want to break the rules learn to route around simple filters by misspelling words, using euphemisms, or relying on shared subtext. A message can be completely clean on the surface and still carry a clear threat, a piece of hate, or an attempt to harass. Implicit moderation is about reading that subtext the way a human moderator would, then flagging it with the same label as the explicit version.

## Real-world examples

- "It would be a real shame if someone showed up at your house tonight." No threatening keyword appears, but the intent is intimidation.
- A dog whistle or number code that stands in for a slur inside a particular community.
- "Great job, genius, really nailed it again" aimed at a specific user, where the literal words are positive but the intent is an insult.
- "You would be better off not being here at all," which can be an indirect encouragement of self-harm.

## Why it matters

Keyword filters and blocklists miss the majority of real abuse because real abuse rarely announces itself. Relying on them produces a false sense of safety: the dashboard looks quiet while targeted users are still being harassed through wording the filter never learned. Implicit moderation closes that gap, which is essential for protecting communities where bad actors adapt quickly. It also reduces over-blocking, because understanding intent means a clinical or quoted use of a hard word is not automatically punished the way a raw blocklist would punish it. Supervisor treats implicit and explicit harm as the same category, so a coded threat and a spelled-out one both land under the same [moderation label](/glossary/moderation-labels).
